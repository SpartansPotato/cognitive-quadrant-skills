# General cognitive workflow

## 1. Identify the task and desired outcome

Summarize the issue in one sentence. Identify why it matters and what the user wants at the end: clarity, a decision, a revised plan, a learning route, an action experiment, or a saved report.

## 2. Build the first quadrant map

### Express

Separate:

- confirmed facts;
- direct experience and observations;
- current interpretations and opinions;
- emotions and values;
- assumptions and predictions;
- previous actions;
- resources and constraints.

### Ask

Convert vague uncertainty into questions. Classify questions as:

- answerable by the user;
- answerable by another stakeholder;
- searchable from external sources;
- testable through action or observation.

Prioritize only questions likely to change the decision or problem definition.

### Iterate

Look for:

- relevant past experience;
- skills or resources the user is underusing;
- contradictions in the narrative;
- repeated patterns;
- unstated evaluation criteria;
- hidden assumptions;
- a problem framed at the wrong level;
- evidence already available but not recognized as evidence.

Present iteration as: original view → overlooked element → revised view → practical consequence.

### Explore

Generate 3–5 directions that could materially alter the analysis:

- another stakeholder's perspective;
- a counterexample;
- a boundary condition;
- a second-order or long-term effect;
- a cross-domain analogy;
- a new technology or environmental change;
- a different problem definition;
- a low-probability, high-impact risk or opportunity.

For each direction, state why it matters and how it could be checked.

## 3. Decide whether external research is needed

Search only when current facts, professional knowledge, competing viewpoints, or high-stakes accuracy matter. Do not search merely to make a reflective response look academic.

## 4. Update cognition explicitly

Use a table when useful:

| Earlier understanding | New information or perspective | Updated understanding | Still uncertain |
|---|---|---|---|

## 5. Convert insight into action

Recommend no more than five next steps. Prefer steps that are:

- low-cost;
- reversible;
- observable;
- time-bounded;
- capable of testing a key assumption.

For each step, state what to do, what it tests, and what result would change the next decision.
