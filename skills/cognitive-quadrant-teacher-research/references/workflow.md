# Teacher research workflow

## 1. Start from practice, not fashionable terminology

Clarify:

- the teaching or professional setting;
- the learners or participants;
- the observable problem;
- current practice and its limitations;
- available materials, data, cases, and partnerships;
- the intended research output.

Separate a broad theme from a researchable problem.

## 2. Build the first quadrant map

### Express

Record facts, teaching experience, existing interventions, observations, data, documents, judgments, and untested assumptions.

### Ask

Create questions that require:

- clarification from the teacher;
- analysis of uploaded material;
- external literature, policy, standard, or industry search;
- empirical verification in future research.

Prioritize questions that determine the research object, mechanism, outcome, or boundary.

### Iterate

Surface resources that teachers often overlook:

- student work and revision traces;
- classroom observation and feedback;
- assessment records;
- enterprise cases and role standards;
- prior course reforms;
- implementation failures;
- tacit teaching strategies;
- contradictions between intended and observed outcomes.

Reframe “Does the technology work?” into more explanatory questions when appropriate: how, why, for whom, under what conditions, and with what unintended effects.

### Explore

Investigate new theories, variables, stakeholders, mechanisms, negative effects, boundary conditions, comparative cases, and cross-disciplinary methods.

## 3. Turn gaps into a search plan

Expand Chinese and English terms. Search for:

- core construct;
- population and educational context;
- mechanism;
- outcomes;
- implementation conditions;
- positive and negative findings;
- related theories;
- methods and measurement approaches.

## 4. Build an evidence map

Use:

| Source or theory | Main claim | Relevance | Source type | Evidence status | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Explain how evidence changes the research problem. Do not produce an unannotated link list.

## 5. Make cognitive revision visible

Use:

| Earlier assumption | External evidence or internal material | Problem found | Revised understanding |
|---|---|---|---|

## 6. Generate research directions

Each direction must include:

- existing basis;
- unresolved problem;
- specific contribution;
- 1–3 research questions;
- feasible evidence and method;
- boundary and risk;
- evidence status.

## 7. End with a feasible research path

Recommend a small number of next steps: define constructs, locate data, select cases, pilot an instrument or coding frame, interview stakeholders, or test a minimal intervention.
